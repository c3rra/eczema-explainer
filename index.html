Atopic dermatitis / a plain-language explainer

How eczema
actually worksTwo versions of the same story

Eczema is not a rash that happens to itch. It is a damaged wall, an immune system overreacting to what comes through it, and an itch that rewires the nerves underneath.

In plain terms The science

Start here

Your skin is built like a brick wall

Flat dead skin cells are the bricks. A greasy mortar sits between them. The wall has one job: keep water in, keep the world out.

A healthy wall

Thick, even mortar. Nothing gets through in either direction.

An eczema wall

Thin, patchy mortar and a missing brick. The wall leaks both ways.

The five words that do all the work

Jargon, translated once

Lipids

Fats and oils. That is the whole definition. The mortar between your skin bricks is made of fat. Ceramides, cholesterol and fatty acids are three kinds of it.

Filaggrin

A protein with two jobs: squash skin cells flat so they stack like proper bricks, and break down into your skin's own built-in moisturiser. Some people inherit a faulty version.

Cytokine

A chemical message between immune cells. Think of it as a text message. One cell shouts "intruder", others respond. That is all a cytokine is.

IL-4, IL-13, IL-31

Names of specific messages. IL stands for interleukin, meaning "between white blood cells". IL-4 and IL-13 say inflame. IL-31 says itch. Numbered because there are dozens.

Type 2 inflammation

One particular mode of immune response, built for parasites, which misfires in allergy. Eczema, asthma and hay fever are the same misfire in skin, lungs and nose.

Staph

Short for Staphylococcus aureus, a common bacterium. Plenty of healthy people carry it. On damaged skin it takes over and fuels the fire.

Three things go wrong at once

Not a sequence. A cycle that feeds itself.

Each one is written twice below: the plain version on the left, the technical version on the right. Same fact, two registers.

Barrier

The wall leaks

In plain terms

The mortar holding your skin bricks together is made of fat, and in eczema it is thin and badly made.

Some people are born with a faulty filaggrin gene. Their bricks are the wrong shape and they make less of their own moisturiser. It is genetic and it runs in families.

The result: water leaks out from the inside, and dust, soap and bacteria get in from the outside.

The science

Your outermost skin layer is corneocytes held together by lipids (ceramides, cholesterol, fatty acids). In eczema that mortar is thin and disorganised.

Many people carry loss-of-function variants in filaggrin, the protein that helps flatten those cells and produce natural moisturising factor.

Water escapes outward, irritants and allergens get in.

Immune

The alarm overreacts

In plain terms

Your immune system is the security team. When something gets through the wall, it sends out chemical messages that redden and swell the area.

Here is the nasty bit. Those same messages also switch off your skin's fat production.

So the wall leaks, stuff gets in, the alarm fires, and the alarm stops you making mortar. The wall gets weaker still.

The science

What gets in triggers a type-2 inflammatory response: IL-4, IL-13, IL-31 and the rest.

These cytokines cause the redness and swelling, and they also suppress the genes that make barrier lipids.

So inflammation degrades the barrier further. That is the loop.

Microbes

The bacteria take advantage

In plain terms

Your skin is covered in millions of bacteria all the time. That is normal and mostly good, because the harmless ones occupy the space and keep troublemakers out.

On eczema skin, staph thrives. The wall is damaged, the normal bacteria are struggling, so staph moves in and releases substances that wind the immune system up further.

More staph means worse flare. Worse flare means more staph.

The science

Eczematous skin gets colonised by Staphylococcus aureus, which crowds out commensals and secretes toxins that drive more inflammation.

Flares and staph density track each other closely.

Why it does not settle on its own

The loop

Moisturising is not a cosmetic afterthought. You are manually replacing mortar your body has been ordered to stop making.

This is why eczema does not simply calm down if left alone, and why treating only when it looks bad means always playing catch-up. The barrier defect is still there between flares.

A separate problem, not a symptom

The itch rewires the nerves underneath

The signal

The message IL-31 lands directly on the nerve endings in your skin and makes them fire "itch". No damage required, just the chemical.

The nerves grow

In long-term eczema, itch nerve endings physically grow longer and reach further up toward the surface. More nerve, closer to the top.

They get twitchy

The threshold drops. A warm room, a jumper, sweat, sometimes nothing at all, now reads as itch.

Scratching backfires

Scratching tears the wall open and releases more itch chemicals. It genuinely makes the itch worse, on a delay.

This is a wiring problem, not a self-control problem. Your nerves have been rewired by months or years of inflammation, so telling yourself to stop is fighting your own nervous system. The practical answer is to make scratching harder rather than relying on willpower: cold on the area, nails short, something else to do with your hands.

Where it commonly goes wrong

The mistakes that cost people months

Two of these waste time. The third can cost you your sight, and it is the one most people have never been warned about.

Hydrocortisone 1%

"I tried steroids. They didn't work for me."

What usually happened

A nervous smear, once a day, stopped the moment the redness faded. The inflammation underneath had not settled, so it rebounded within days.

Or the strength was wrong for the place. Over-the-counter 1% is a mild steroid. It is often too weak for thick skin like hands, elbows and shins, where a stronger one is the correct choice.

Or it was applied to dry, unmoisturised skin, so it was fighting a broken barrier the whole time.

What actually works

Enough to leave the skin glistening, and continued for a few days after it looks clear, because the inflammation outlasts the redness.

Dose by fingertip unit: a line squeezed from the tip of an adult finger to the first crease covers roughly two flat palms of skin.

Keep moisturising alongside it, not instead of it. Once settled, applying twice a week to the spots that always flare prevents the next one better than waiting.

Fear of steroids causes more harm than the steroids do. Under-treating leaves inflammation running, and it is prolonged inflammation that thickens skin and rewires the itch nerves.

Sudocrem

The cream in every bathroom cupboard, aimed at a different problem.

Why it disappoints

It was built for nappy rash and minor grazes. The main ingredient, zinc oxide, is drying and astringent. That suits a weeping nappy rash and works directly against dry, cracked eczema.

It also contains lanolin and several fragrance-related ingredients, including benzyl benzoate and benzyl cinnamate. All are recognised causes of contact allergy.

On skin whose barrier is already broken, those ingredients reach the immune system far more easily, so the odds of reacting to it are higher than on normal skin.

Where it earns its place

Short term, on a raw or weeping patch, as a physical barrier while the skin closes over. That is a real job and it does it well.

For everyday eczema, a plain ceramide cream and an ointment do more, and contain nothing to react to.

Its reputation comes from being familiar rather than from being right for this. Never use it on eyelids.

The serious one

Steroid creams on eyelids can damage your eyes

Eyelid skin is around half a millimetre thick, the thinnest on the body. A steroid that is unremarkable on an elbow behaves very differently there, because far more of it passes straight through. Cream also migrates along the lid margin and into the eye itself.

Raised pressure inside the eye

Steroids can push up the fluid pressure in the eyeball. Roughly one person in three responds this way, and there is no way to know in advance whether you are one of them.

Glaucoma

Sustained pressure damages the optic nerve, the cable carrying sight to the brain. It is painless and silent until vision is already going, and the loss does not come back.

Cataract

Prolonged steroid exposure is linked to clouding at the back of the lens, blurring vision and causing glare around lights.

Thinning skin

The lid becomes fragile, crepey and papery, with small blood vessels showing through. Thin skin is the exact opposite of what eczema treatment is for.

What to use instead

This is precisely why calcineurin inhibitors exist. Tacrolimus and pimecrolimus calm the same inflammation without thinning skin and without raising eye pressure, which is what made them the standard choice for eyelids. Both are prescription only, so eyelid eczema is a reason to see a doctor rather than a shelf to solve it from.

They commonly sting or burn for the first few days, then stop. People abandon them on day two believing they are reacting to them.

This is not an argument against steroids. Doctors do prescribe short, supervised courses of mild steroid on eyelids, and they work. The danger is a potent one, used unsupervised, for weeks. Note that over-the-counter hydrocortisone boxes carry an explicit warning against use on the face and near the eyes.

The one sentence version

Your skin wall is built with weak mortar, so things get in, your immune system overreacts to them, and its overreaction stops you making more mortar.

Everything you do is either patching the wall, calming the alarm, or breaking the itch.

Built by Chris Cerra, lifelong eczema survivor.

Not a substitute for a GP or dermatologist, particularly on prescription choice and dosing, and especially for eyelids where getting it wrong has consequences for the eyes.
